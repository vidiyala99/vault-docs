KEEPING SPRINKLERED WAREHOUSES ACTUALLY PROTECTED
A primer for risk managers

Automatic sprinklers remain the single most effective fixed protection
for warehouse occupancies, but a sprinkler system only earns its credit
when it matches the commodities stored beneath it. The majority of
large-loss warehouse fires in sprinklered buildings involve a mismatch:
storage was changed after the system was designed.

DESIGN BASIS AND COMMODITY CREEP

Every sprinkler system is designed against a specific commodity
classification, storage height, and storage arrangement. When a tenant
switches from palletized canned goods to plastic housewares, the fire
load can double or triple, while the ceiling density the system can
deliver stays fixed. Risk managers should re-validate the design basis
whenever stored commodities, rack heights, or aisle widths change.
Plastics stored above 25 feet in racks generally require in-rack
sprinklers or an ESFR ceiling-only design.

INSPECTION, TESTING, AND MAINTENANCE

Sprinkler impairments are the second leading driver of large warehouse
fire losses. A closed control valve can quietly defeat an otherwise
adequate system. Control valves should be locked open and inspected
weekly. Dry-pipe systems need annual trip tests, and fire pumps require
weekly churn tests plus an annual flow test. Impairment management
matters most: any planned shutdown of fire protection should follow a
permit process with the insurer notified and hot work suspended while
protection is out of service.

HOT WORK AND IGNITION CONTROL

Cutting and welding remain the leading ignition source in warehouse
fires. A hot work permit program should require a 35-foot clear zone,
a dedicated fire watch during the work, and a 60-minute monitoring
period after the work is complete. Battery charging stations for
forklifts should be located against non-combustible walls with one
hour of separation from rack storage.

HOUSEKEEPING AND IDLE PALLETS

Idle wooden pallets are among the fastest-developing fire loads in any
warehouse. Outdoor pallet storage should be at least 50 feet from the
building wall. Indoor idle pallet stacks should be limited to six feet
in height unless the sprinkler design specifically contemplates higher
stacking.

WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE

A well-run warehouse program pairs quarterly self-inspections with an
annual engineering visit, keeps the sprinkler design basis documented
and posted, and treats every impairment as a managed event rather than
a maintenance afterthought. Facilities that follow these practices see
materially lower loss frequency, and when fires do occur, they are far
more likely to be controlled by the first few sprinkler heads.
